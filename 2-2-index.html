Examples of Successful Activism

We compiled a list of successful youth-led activism from around the world. Take a look at these examples for inspiration for your own projects!

Teens Who Care

When the COVID-19 pandemic first hit, high school freshman Sameeksha Dangi grew eager to help her community; but after spending hours fruitlessly looking for volunteer opportunities, she couldn’t believe how difficult it was to find ways to give back. So, she decided to create one.

Sameeksha visualized having a non-profit website dedicated to making volunteering accessible to teens. To make her vision a reality, she researched, planned, communicated, and even hired a web designer to help create the website. Her work didn’t stop there--she then made sure to spread the word through PeachJar fliers, word-of-mouth, and Google Advertising. But most importantly, she maintained her long-term vision throughout all of the setbacks she faced.

Teens Who Care has since provided volunteer opportunities to over 6,500 teens, who have helped over 6,500 people!

Learn more at: https://teenswhocare.us/

Lake Stevens SACo (Student Advisory Council)

After struggling with feeling like her voice isn’t heard in the school system, Kaitlyn Johnson from Lake Stevens, Washington wanted to create a way for students to be involved in decision-making for her school district. So with the help of a couple of friends, she reached out to the Lake Stevens School board and created SACo (Student Advisory Council). This is a board of about 10 students who meet every month to discuss issues in the school district and how to address them in the best way possible for the students. They present their ideas and participate at the district’s board meetings to ensure that the input of the youth in the district is being heard by the staff that does the decision-making. Even though it has only been an active group for about a year now, the Lake Stevens SACo has already made changes for the better.

Learn more at: https://lshsvalhalla.com/4220/news/students-find-their-voices/

Joshua´s Heart Foundation

After watching a TV commercial depicting children starving around the world, 4-year-old Joshua Williams knew that he had to do something to help. With the support of his family, he started Joshua’s Heart Foundation, a non-profit organization with a clear mission of ”Stomping Out World Hunger”. His activism started out small, cooking and handing out meals to local people in need. And as the organization grew in members, its impact grew as well.

Since being founded in 2005, Joshua’s Heart Foundation has distributed over 4.8 million pounds of food, helped over 600,000 people, and raised over 3 million dollars.

Learn more at: https://joshuasheart.org/

Maria Marshall (Little Thoughts On Big Matters Video)

After seeing a person littering, Maria Marshall (a 12-year-old from Barbados) decided she wanted to make a difference by creating an award-winning short film called Little Thoughts on Big Matters which focuses on the concept of recycling, reusing, reducing, and repurposing. Since the success of her first YouTube video, Maria has continued to create short films that encourage and inform people around the world about different topics, especially ones that will make a change in the world.

March For Our Lives

On February 14th, 2018, a shooting took place at Marjory Stoneman Douglas High that claimed the lives of 17 people. Several of its survivors--including David Hogg, Emma Gonzalez, and Cameron Kasky--met up in the days that followed to create a strategy to prevent similar tragedies from happening. This is how they founded March For Our Lives: an organization devoted to protesting gun violence and to encouraging gun control laws. The students made speeches, appeared on television, and used social media to garner support. After only five weeks of swift, but careful planning, the students were able to gather over 800,000 people to march in Washington D.C., and they inspired over 800 global sister marches.

Learn more at: https://marchforourlives.com/mission-story/

1,000 Black Girl Books

Marley Dias, a 13-year-old, felt very frustrated with the lack of diversity in books she was reading in school. After realizing this frustration, Dias came up with the idea to start a book drive called #1000blackgirlbooks which is a drive that collects books featuring black females as the main character. Now, she has surpassed the 1,000 books goal and has now collected over 11,000 books which she hopes will not only bring more diversity to the books we read but also serve as more inspiring figures for black girls to look up to.

Learn more at: https://www.pbs.org/newshour/arts/13-year-old-founder-of-1000blackgirlbooks-shares-some-of-her-favorite-reads

The Sunrise Movement

The Sunrise Movement is a movement organized for youth that focuses on putting an end to climate change all while creating millions of jobs for people. They have created a large group of youth that are all working towards the same goal of making climate change a priority and taking the steps to put an end to it. They currently have organized many different events and campaigns for young people who are passionate about this topic to participate in.

Learn more at: https://www.sunrisemovement.org/about/

Sierra Student Coalition

The Sierra Club is a community built nationally that has students and young people from ages 14 to 35. This group specifically focuses on organizing events for climate change, racial, and economic justice. They organize events, create leadership opportunities, form campaigns, educate, work with other organizations, and so much more! So far they have organized 4,500 events and gotten 3M+ petition signatures. They have been helping students learn more and become successful advocates since 1991.

Learn more at: https://www.sierraclub.org/youth/about

United We Dream

Throughout the early 2000s, more and more youth activists were inspired to join the fight to expand education access and legalization for undocumented immigrants. However, it wasn’t until 2007 that many of these activists decided to hold a national gathering to discuss issues related to their cause. At this gathering, they decided to create a powerful and united youth movement. Their goal was to create an organization that was both sustainable and engaging for immigrant youths. This is how United We Dream began. Since then, its over 700,000 members have continued to advocate for immigrants, and their campaigns have led to DACA being enacted!

Learn more at: https://unitedwedream.org/

Fridays For Future (FFF)

At just 15 years old, Greta Thunberg was determined to spark action regarding climate change. So in August of 2018, she decided to organize a strike at her school to encourage the Swedish Parliament to take action. But what began as a local effort, became a global effort. Thunberg spread her message through social media by starting #FridaysForFuture, and the hashtag quickly spread over many platforms. On the 15th of March, 2019, over a million young people, from 125 different countries, participated in over 2,200 strikes. Since that first strike, Fridays For Future has organized many more, similarly successful strikes. FFF’s efforts have helped millions of young people to stand up for their planet and have kindled an increased awareness of climate issues.

Learn more at: https://fridaysforfuture.org/
